# Beat & Editorial Calendar

## Beat Definition

[PUBLICATION NAME] covers the human texture of business — specifically, the encounters, conversations, relationships, and forms of memory that constitute professional life as a lived experience.

The animating questions:

- What does it actually take for two strangers to become trusted colleagues?
- What forms of memory and attention sustain long professional relationships?
- What is happening, anthropologically, when we shake a hand, exchange a card, share a meal, or remember a name?
- What is being lost, and what is being preserved, as more of business moves to screens?
- Who are the masters of in-person commerce, and what do they know?

## Coverage Lanes

1. **The Anthropology of Professional Encounter** — trade shows, conferences, sales calls, business lunches, networking events as cultural practices with histories and rituals.
2. **Memory and Relationship** — the role of remembering in sustaining trust; the tools and disciplines people develop to remember; the social cost of forgetting.
3. **Trust Between Strangers** — how it forms, how it breaks, what it depends on. Reporting on contexts where this question is acute (door-to-door sales, diplomacy, hospitality, deal-making).
4. **The Disappearing Arts** — practices that defined business for generations and are now changing or fading: the long lunch, the handshake deal, the regular client visit, the holiday card.
5. **Profiles** — masters of in-person commerce. The salesperson who's been with the same accounts for thirty years. The hotel concierge who remembers everyone. The deal-maker, the host, the connector.
6. **History** — commerce as an ancient human activity. The long view: medieval trade fairs, the gift economies that preceded markets, the role of feasts and festivals in commerce.
7. **Contrast Pieces** — what changes when business moves to Zoom, when CRM replaces memory, when LinkedIn replaces the alumni network. Honest, not nostalgic.

## Out of Scope (firm)

- Conversation intelligence, AI notetakers, sales engagement platforms, CRM software, RevOps tooling
- Reviews of any sales technology product
- Any company in which the founder has a commercial interest
- Generic "future of work" content
- Hot takes on remote vs. in-person work
- Anything that reads as thinly disguised advocacy for Overclock or its category

## 12-Piece Editorial Calendar

The first twelve pieces, sequenced for archive-building. Each has an angle, primary form, voice register, and notes on sources.

### Launch Strategy: Three Pieces in Week 1

The publication launches with three pieces published simultaneously, then moves to a one-per-week cadence. The launch trio is the first three pieces below — chosen because they are library essays (no reporting blockers), span two voice registers, and together form a coherent thematic opening: memory as the foundation of relationship, the physical objects we use to support it, the reciprocal obligation it creates.

**Week 1:** Pieces 1, 2, 3 published together
**Weeks 2–10:** One piece per week, in order, with adjustments as reporting permits

### LAUNCH TRIO

### Piece 1: "The Long Memory of Strangers"
- **Form:** Essay
- **Register:** Atlantic
- **Length:** ~2,200 words
- **Publish:** Week 1 (launch)
- **Angle:** The flagship piece. Argues that what we call "business relationships" are, at their core, mutual acts of remembering — and that the sophistication of professional culture is in large part a sophistication about memory. Ranges from anthropology (gift economies, the kula ring) through 19th-century commercial practice (the merchant's ledger, the calling card) to a tentative argument about what's at stake now. Sets the publication's intellectual register.
- **Sources:** Library piece. No interviews required.

### Piece 2: "The Card and the Name"
- **Form:** Short essay
- **Register:** Gladwell
- **Length:** ~1,400 words
- **Publish:** Week 1 (launch)
- **Angle:** A short, punchy essay on the business card — what it is, what it does, why it has survived every prediction of its death. Anecdote-driven. Could include the history of the calling card, *meishi* etiquette in Japan, the texture of cards (a Patrick Bateman beat, used judiciously). Lands on a real argument about why the physical object persists.
- **Sources:** Library; 1–2 light quotes optional but not required for launch.

### Piece 3: "What We Owe the Person Who Remembered Us"
- **Form:** Essay
- **Register:** Atlantic
- **Length:** ~1,800 words
- **Publish:** Week 1 (launch)
- **Angle:** On the reciprocal obligation that being remembered creates. The guest list, the alumni note, the "still thinking about you" email — the structure of social debt that long memory produces. Lightly philosophical, drawing from Mauss on the gift. Ends with an observation about what the modern professional version of this looks like, and what it doesn't.
- **Sources:** Library piece.

### WEEKLY CADENCE

### Piece 4: "The Long Lunch"
- **Form:** Essay
- **Register:** Atlantic
- **Length:** ~2,000 words
- **Publish:** Week 2
- **Angle:** On the decline and partial survival of the multi-hour business lunch. Argues that what looks like inefficiency was actually a sophisticated form of professional infrastructure — and that what's replaced it (the coffee meeting, the Zoom call) doesn't quite do the same work. Avoid easy nostalgia: the long lunch was also exclusionary, often boozy, and not always good for those who weren't already inside.
- **Sources:** A few interviews — older deal-makers who remember; perhaps a hospitality historian.

### Piece 5: "On Forgetting Names"
- **Form:** Short essay
- **Register:** Gladwell
- **Length:** ~1,500 words
- **Publish:** Week 3
- **Angle:** A short, observational essay on the small social violence of forgetting someone's name — and the techniques humans have developed to avoid it. Memory aids from antiquity (the loci method) to mnemonic apps. Light, but with a point about what it means to be the kind of person who is good at this.
- **Sources:** Light reporting; one or two memory experts.

### Piece 6: "The Trade Fair, From Champagne to Hannover"
- **Form:** History/essay
- **Register:** Atlantic
- **Length:** ~2,800 words
- **Publish:** Week 4
- **Angle:** The eight-hundred-year history of the trade fair. From the Champagne fairs of medieval France through Leipzig and Hannover to CES and Dreamforce. What has changed, what hasn't, what the persistence of this format tells us about how humans do business. Surprising claim: the trade fair has been declared dead at every technological transition for 600 years and has never died. Why?
- **Sources:** Mostly historical/secondary; one contemporary fair organizer would strengthen it.

### Piece 7: "Notes from the Floor: Small Business Expo NYC"
- **Form:** Reportage
- **Register:** Mixed
- **Length:** ~2,500 words
- **Publish:** Week 5
- **Angle:** Your Small Business Expo trip (May 7), written as literary reporting. **Don't make it about Overclock — make it about the room.** What is a small-business expo, anthropologically? Who's there, what are they doing, what does the floor sound like at hour seven? What are the small dramas — the booth that's drowning, the booth that's printing money, the duo who've worked these expos for twenty years? Use the trip to file the piece.
- **Sources:** Multiple attendees (named); ideally one veteran exhibitor with stories.
- **Logistics:** This is your first reporting trip. Bring a notebook, not a sales pitch. Talk to people about their work, not yours.

### Piece 8: "What the Concierge Knows"
- **Form:** Profile
- **Register:** New Yorker
- **Length:** ~3,500 words
- **Publish:** Week 6
- **Angle:** A long-form profile of a senior hotel concierge — ideally at a Boston institution like the Liberty, the Fairmont Copley, or the Eliot — who has spent 25+ years remembering returning guests. Watch them work for a day. Explore what they do, how they do it, and what they understand about strangers and trust that the rest of us miss.
- **Sources:** Concierge (named); 1–2 colleagues; 1–2 returning guests if accessible.
- **Lead-time:** Begin outreach week 1. This piece needs ~5 weeks of runway.

### Piece 9: "Profile: The Outside Salesperson"
- **Form:** Profile
- **Register:** Gladwell (with New Yorker patience for scene)
- **Length:** ~3,000 words
- **Publish:** Week 7
- **Angle:** A profile of a veteran outside-sales rep — someone who has worked the same territory for fifteen-plus years. Industrial supplies, medical devices, commercial real estate, restaurant equipment — the kinds of fields where the rep *is* the relationship. Spend a day in their car. Cover the rituals: the coffee at the start of every visit, the family questions, the kids' birthdays remembered.
- **Sources:** Named subject; customers if accessible; possibly the subject's manager.
- **Lead-time:** Begin outreach week 2.

### Piece 10: "The Connector"
- **Form:** Profile
- **Register:** New Yorker
- **Length:** ~3,000 words
- **Publish:** Week 8
- **Angle:** A profile of someone in your extended network who is famously good at introducing people — a partner at a firm, a community organizer, a fixer. Not "the supernode" pop-science take. The actual texture of what they do, what they don't do, how they think about it. The economy of introductions.
- **Sources:** Named subject; 3–4 people they've connected.
- **Lead-time:** Begin outreach week 3.

### Piece 11: "A Day at the Boston Wholesale Flower Market"
- **Form:** Reportage
- **Register:** Mixed
- **Length:** ~2,500 words
- **Publish:** Week 9
- **Angle:** Pre-dawn at the Boston Flower Exchange. The relationships between buyers and sellers, the credit lines built on no contracts, the multi-generational pairs. A meditation on what cash-flow trust looks like in trades where everything depends on long memory and reputation. Smell, light, the rituals of the early hours.
- **Sources:** Multiple traders; observable multi-year relationships.
- **Lead-time:** Begin outreach week 5.

### Piece 12: "The Year in In-Person"
- **Form:** Capstone essay
- **Register:** Atlantic
- **Length:** ~2,000 words
- **Publish:** Week 10
- **Angle:** A capstone piece. Survey the publication's first run and synthesize. What patterns emerged? What did you learn that you didn't expect? What's the through-line? Sets up the second year and signals editorial maturity.
- **Sources:** Self-referential — your own previous pieces.
